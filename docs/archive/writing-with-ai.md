# Writing with AI: 5 Steps to Success

> Source: University of St. Gallen — Official guidelines on AI use in academic work.

---

## 1. Is using AI generally allowed?

Yes. At the University of St.Gallen, we expect that you are thoughtful, honest and transparent about any use of generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) technologies, especially in your academic work. Generally, the University allows you to use generative AI tools such as ChatGPT, Claude, Elicit, or DeepL, as long as you use them to support your writing, not do it for you. You may generally use artificial intelligence in two ways: as an aid (must be listed as an aid) or as part of your argumentation (requires proper in-text citation and a detailed entry in the reference list); refer to Directives of the Dean of Studies, section 5.1.1.

That said, independent thought and engagement with a specific topic within a given timeframe remain essential requirements for scholarly work. Your work is still required to adhere to the standards of academic rigour: conclusions must be supported by research findings, data, and facts; they must be intersubjectively verifiable, transparent, objectively presented, clearly articulated, critically examined, honest, original, and logically reasoned (cf. Gröner, 2025).

When using AI outputs, always critically evaluate and double-check them, as you would with any source. Keep also in mind that faculty members may limit the use of AI or specify rules for its use in the course fact sheet (e.g., requiring you to submit utilised prompts and AI responses). When in doubt, consult your supervising faculty member.

## 2. What is permitted and what is not?

### Not permitted

- **Unacknowledged AI use.** Any use of AI that is not properly acknowledged. Submitting written work without properly acknowledging your use of AI may be considered as academic dishonesty according to Art. 38 ER BL [II.C.01] / Art. 40 ER ML [II.D.01]. In cases of suspected unacknowledged third-party work, your supervisor may apply a grade deduction (down to a grade of 1).

- **AI as a source for facts.** Relying on AI as a source for facts or scientific conclusions. AI chatbots, akin to Wikipedia, are not regarded as reliable or verifiable sources. Accordingly, AI does not replace a thorough literature review using the resources provided by the HSG Library.

- **Distorting summaries/paraphrases.** Using AI to summarise or paraphrase your own or someone else's text where this results in distortion of the original statement or introduces erroneous claims that you then adopt. With or without AI, you are responsible as the author for ensuring the accuracy of your content.

- **Hiding AI-generated paraphrases.** Using AI to summarise or paraphrase others' texts or information while only citing the original source. Summarising and paraphrasing scientific material in your own words is an essential independent academic achievement and a core element of scholarly discourse. Consequently, any AI-generated summaries or paraphrases must be clearly distinguishable from your own writing as well as properly documented and cited according to standard citation norms. A mention in the list of aids is insufficient.

- **Feeding AI with protected data.** Do not supply AI applications with data protected by privacy laws (e.g., health records, personal or confidential information like interview transcripts, etc.) or copyrighted materials without explicit consent. Use public data or information that has been cleared for sharing. As the author, you are liable for any breaches of data protection or copyright laws.

### Permitted

- **Inspiration and idea generation.** Using AI for inspiration, idea generation, and as a starting point for your own reflections. However, please note: the quality of the responses depends on the quality of your prompts and the availability of relevant information in the training dataset.
  > List as an aid

- **AI-generated texts/images as objects of analysis.** For example, you might want to compare AI-generated job applications with those written by humans. Use in-text citation, e.g., (OpenAI, 2024), including the prompts as the basis for your analysis and argumentation. Specify the AI software used in your reference list. Include the AI output in the appendix.
  > Provide in-text citation and reference

- **AI-generated images and graphics for illustration.** You may use AI-generated images and charts for illustrative purposes. Cite the respective AI in the text and reference it.
  > Provide in-text citation and reference

- **AI as part of the research method (if justified).** It may be appropriate to handle certain academic routine tasks with the help of AI; for example, extracting specific information categories from very large datasets and/or paraphrasing these categories, such as for extensive meta-analysis. Similarly, it may be useful to have code generated or improved by AI. However, you are required to make it clearly evident what is AI-generated and what is your own contribution. Describe and justify the use of AI, including the prompts, in your methods section. Add the AI to the list of aids. If the content generated by AI appears in your text, cite the AI in the text and add it to your reference list.
  > List as an aid, provide in-text citation and reference if applicable

- **Transcribing audio or video material.** Ideally, use an AI tool specifically developed for this task. It must be privacy-compliant and suitable for use with confidential data.
  > List as an aid

- **Translations to understand source text.** You can use AI to translate a source text into the language you are most proficient in.
  > List as an aid

- **Translations in the text.** For example, you may translate individual words or a verbatim quote using AI. In the first case, use it as a dictionary; in the latter, as a human translator. In both cases, list the AI used as an aid. If you use AI to translate a direct quote, your in-text citation must show clearly that the translation was generated by AI, e.g., (Lange, 2017, p. 187, translated with DeepL).
  > List as an aid

- **Linguistic revision of your own text.** Provided no content distortions or factual inaccuracies arise (e.g., through repeated simplification). Specialised tools (e.g., Grammarly or DeepL Write) are better suited for this than ChatGPT, as they alter the content less and generate fewer unwanted, unsubstantiated, and imprecise interpretations. Note: a simple prompt for rephrasing submitted to ChatGPT often leads to lower linguistic quality, which can be mistaken for improved readability, especially by non-native writers. Carefully check the accuracy of statements and the use of technical terms. For verification and backup purposes, save a copy of your text before having it rephrased.
  > List as an aid

- **Paraphrasing others' texts.** Provided the original statement is preserved, and the paraphrase is identifiable as AI-generated in your citation. However, please note: a tool based on large language models, such as ChatGPT, cannot guarantee "no content changes" in the human sense. Verify this meticulously using the necessary expertise, particularly the correct use of technical terms. Be aware that multiple paraphrasings and rephrasings through AI can lead to unintended plagiarism, e.g., through:
  - excessive simplification, which leads to distortion of the original statement
  - excessive proximity to the source text (only a few words are changed, so that the text no longer qualifies as a paraphrase)
  - mixing of others' and your own text parts (this distorts the content of the paraphrased quote and makes it unclear what is external and what is your own contribution)

  For verification and backup purposes, always save a copy of your text before having it rephrased. This use of AI, including the relevant prompt, must be cited in the text, and full details provided in your reference list.
  > Provide in-text citation and reference

## 3. Where in the text do I have to cite the use of AI?

HSG differentiates between two ways of using the AI:

1. **AI as an aid**
2. **AI as part of your argumentation**

In the first case, add the corresponding AI to the list of aids used. In the second case, you must quote the AI either verbatim or paraphrased and provide in-text citation as well as reference it.

If you use AI as part of your research method, describe and justify the use in your method section. State the AI tool used in the list of aids. If you use the AI-generated content in your text (paraphrased or verbatim), provide an in-text citation and a corresponding entry in your reference list.

For proper classification (aid or part of your argumentation?), please refer to the types of AI use described in section 2.

## 4. How do I create a correct in-text citation and a reference?

University of St.Gallen follows primarily the **APA referencing style**. In the following, it is demonstrated — using ChatGPT as an example — how to correctly cite AI according to APA. You may follow other styles as well. If you are unsure which style to follow, consult your supervisor.

### In-text citation (APA style)

The APA recommends that you describe in the methods or introduction section of your work how and for what exactly you used AI, especially if you use AI as part of your research method. In general, make sure to mention the prompts you used wherever possible — this ensures transparency.

**Example 1: Direct quote**

> When prompted with "Write a formal invitation text for a lecture about endurance sports in old age," ChatGPT generated the following text: "Dear Sir or Madam, We are pleased to invite you to an enlightening lecture titled 'Endurance Sports in Old Age: Benefits and Best Practices' [...]" (OpenAI, 2025).

**Example 2: Paraphrased text**

> According to ChatGPT's response to the prompt "What are the most important virtues today?", empathy, responsibility, honesty, and integrity are currently considered particularly important and desirable in our society (OpenAI, 2025).

**Example 3: Image generated by AI**

> Figure 1: White horse wearing a hat (source: Leonardo.ai (2024); prompt: "Draw a picture of a white horse wearing a hat")

### Referencing (APA style)

An entry in the reference list must include the following elements (based on ChatGPT as an example):

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Author** | State the developing company (here OpenAI) as the author |
| **Date** | Specify the year of the version you used, not the exact date |
| **Title** | The name of the AI model is "ChatGPT." The name serves as the title and is italicised. There is no need to specify the unique iteration, i.e. ChatGPT-3 or ChatGPT-4 |
| **Version** | Indicate the date of the version used in parentheses, i.e. (June 1 version) |
| **Description** | Add a description of the tool in square brackets, in this case [Large language model] |
| **Source** | Provide the URL of the generative AI tool |

**Examples:**

```
OpenAI. (2023). ChatGPT (Oct 1 version) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com/chat.

Leonardo Interactive (2024). Leonardo.ai (June 1 version) [Large language model]. https://app.leonardo.ai/.
```

## 5. How do I list AI as an aid?

Non-generative tools such as spelling or grammar checking programmes, online dictionaries, library catalogues, etc., do not need to be cited in the text but must be added to the list of aids. If you use generative AI as an aid, add it to the list of aids as well (refer to section 3).

**Example:**

| Aid / Tool | Purpose |
|------------|---------|
| Elicit | Support with research work |
| SPSS | Statistical data analysis |
| Lektorat | Editing carried out by the company 'Huber' |
| DeepL Write | Reformulations throughout the thesis |
| ChatGPT | Spell check |
